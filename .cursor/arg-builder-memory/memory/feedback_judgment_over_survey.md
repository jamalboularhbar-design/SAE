**Rule:** When asked to weigh competing views or options, deliver the synthesized judgment, not a survey. Pick a side, name the why, name what would flip the call.

**Why:** Mid-session he sent a counter-audit alongside my audit and asked literally *"what's your honest pro judgment when you mix the two audits?"* — not "summarize both," not "what are the tradeoffs." He'd read both and wanted a take. The same pattern repeated on Option A (templates-first cash path) vs Option B (cohort + Nexus OS) — he wanted a recommendation, not a comparison.

**How to apply:**

Structure for any "what do you think" / "judgment" / "which one" prompt:

1. **Opening line = the verdict.** Not the methodology. Example: *"After reading both audits side by side — the counter-audit is sharper on what you have, mine is sharper on what you're walking into, and the actual decision is which business you're running."*
2. **Where the other view is sharper than mine** (give the other side its due, specifically).
3. **Where mine holds** (defend the parts that are right).
4. **The decision that's actually on the table** — frame it as the underlying choice, not the surface choice.
5. **The recommendation** in one sentence: "Run B. Use Nexus OS as the headline, not as a perk."
6. **What would change the view** — specific signals that would flip the recommendation (cleared trademark, 5 named pre-committed buyers, etc.).

**Watch-outs:**

- Don't pad with "of course this depends on your priorities." It always does. Pick assuming his stated priorities.
- Don't pretend equivalence. If A is meaningfully better than B, say so. He won't reward false balance.
- Don't avoid uncomfortable conclusions. The brand-collision recommendation (drop NexusAI parent) was uncomfortable because he had invested significantly in it — and that was exactly the call to make. Hedging on it would've been worse than naming it.
- If you genuinely don't know, say "I don't have enough to call this — here's what I'd need to see" — not a menu.

**The closing-line pattern he responds to:**
> *"You're not picking between two audits — you're picking between two businesses, and the one you should pick is the one where Nexus OS is the headline and the founder is the moat."*

One sentence. Names the real frame. Takes a side. Use this shape.
